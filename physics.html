1. Explain Photoelectric Emission

Photoelectric emission occurs when light of a certain frequency (or higher) hits the surface of a material, typically a metal, causing the ejection of electrons from that surface. The key points to remember are:

  • Incident Light: Light must have a frequency above a certain threshold for photoelectric emission to occur.
  • Photoelectrons: The ejected electrons are called photoelectrons.
  • Energy Transfer: The energy from the incident photons (light particles) is transferred to electrons in the material. If this energy exceeds the material's work function (the minimum energy needed to remove an electron), an electron is emitted.

2. Calculate:

(i) Threshold Frequency

Given:

  • Work function (\(W\)) = 2.5 eV
  • Planckâ€™s constant (\(h\)) = \(6.6 \times 10^{-34}\) Js
  • 1 eV = \(1.6 \times 10^{-19}\) J

Convert the work function into joules:

\(W = 2.5 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} = 4.0 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}\)

Threshold frequency \(f_0\) is calculated using:

\(f_0 = \frac{W}{h} = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}}{6.6 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{Js}}\)

\(f_0 \approx 6.06 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz}\)

(ii) Maximum Kinetic Energy of Liberated Electrons

Given:

  • Wavelength of incident light (\(\lambda\)) = \(3.5 \times 10^{-7}\) m
  • Speed of light (\(c\)) = \(3.0 \times 10^8\) m/s
  • Planck's constant (\(h\)) = \(6.6 \times 10^{-34}\) Js
  • Work function (\(W\)) = \(4.0 \times 10^{-19}\) J

Calculate the frequency of the light:

\(f = \frac{c}{\lambda} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^8 \, \text{m/s}}{3.5 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{m}} \approx 8.57 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz}\)

Energy of the incident photons (\(hf\)):

\(E_{\text{photon}} = h \times f = 6.6 \times 10^{-34} \, \text{Js} \times 8.57 \times 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \approx 5.66 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}\)

Maximum kinetic energy (\(K_{\text{max}}\)):

\(K_{\text{max}} = E_{\text{photon}} - W = 5.66 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} - 4.0 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J} \approx 1.66 \times 10^{-19} \, \text{J}\)

3. Define the Term Radioactivity and List Three Types of Radiation

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or electromagnetic radiation from the unstable nucleus of certain atoms. This process transforms the original atom into a different element or isotope and is an inherent property of some elements such as uranium, radium, and plutonium.

Three Types of Radiation:

  • Alpha Radiation (Î±): Consists of 2 protons and 2 neutrons (a helium nucleus). It has low penetration power and can be stopped by a sheet of paper or human skin. It has a +2 charge.
  • Beta Radiation (Î²): Composed of high-energy, high-speed electrons (Î²-) or positrons (Î²+). It has moderate penetration power and can be stopped by a few millimeters of aluminum or plastic. Electrons have a -1 charge, while positrons have a +1 charge.
  • Gamma Radiation (Î³): High-energy electromagnetic waves with very high penetration power. Dense materials like lead or several centimeters of concrete are required for effective shielding. Gamma rays have no charge.
